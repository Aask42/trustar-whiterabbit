How Does It Work
  1. Collect Bitcoin addresses observed with ransomware binaries. These Bitcoin addresses are known as seed addresses. They are the Bitcoin addresses used to collect money from the victims.
  2. Leverage the most up to date Bitcoin blockchain information to reconstruct the clusters of Bitcoin addresses associated to the same ransomware family. We computed these clusters by running python3 compute_clusters_main.py from our Github repository.
  3. The balance of the cluster with its peaks and valleys can be used as an indicator of money flowing to, or being cashed out by, the criminals. Because we know with a high certainty that these clusters are associated with the ransomware family, security operators can, by determining that certain cluster balances are trending up, assume with high fidelity that a new variant of certain ransomware type is ramping up.
  4. The amount of money paid to the bad actors can also give the operator a sense of the severity of the attack. Usually, victims pay the criminals when they are desperate to recover their data, i.e., the higher the severity the more money will be paid. Once security operators determine which ransomware family is trending up they can be on the lookout and focus on collecting other indicator types associated to that ransomware.
WhiteRabbit: Follow the Rabbit Hole

WhiteRabbit is an open source tool that allows security professionals to associate Bitcoin addresses to known malware campaigns and track payments made to these addresses. We are aiming to provide a tool that acts as an early warning system that leverages the Bitcoin public, immutable ledger (transaction) data. Given the rise of prominence of cryptocurrencies in general and within the security context, we propose that Bitcoin (and other cryptocurrencies) addresses could be added as indicators to the “Pyramid of Pain” for an analyst, as it shares characteristics with both hashes (trivial -- botom of the pyramid) and TTPs (tough -- top of the pyramid). Read more in our blog post and check out our Github Repository.

Get Started
  1. Click on a Malware Family on the left panel of Ransomware Campaigns.
  2. On the main panel, you'll find the historical balances of the wallet associated with the ransomware campaign's attacker.
  3. The two graphs under Historical Balance of All Clusters visualize the balances of all the clusters of BTC addresses that make up the wallet. The x-axis represents time across the duration of the wallet's activity. For the first graph the y-axis represents the balance of each individual cluster in U.S. Dollars. Due to the volatility in price of Bitcoin over the last few years, our other graphs measure the balance in BTC.
  4. The following graphs under Historical Balance of Individual Clusters separately visualize the balances of each of those individual clusters of BTC addresses that make up the wallet. The x-axis represents time across the duration of the wallet's activity. The y-axis represents the balance of that individual cluster in BTC. For some campaigns with multiple clusters, e.g. Cryptowall, we noticed that each cluster's balances decreased as a following cluster's balances started to increase.
